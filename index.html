⚛️ Uranium — U-235 & U-238
Uranium is element 92. Natural uranium is 99.3% U-238 (non-fissile) and 0.7% U-235 (fissile — capable of sustaining a chain reaction). U-235 has a half-life of 703 million years. U-238 has a half-life of 4.47 billion years — approximately the age of Earth.

Enrichment increases the concentration of U-235. Reactor-grade uranium is 3–5% U-235. Weapons-grade requires 90%+ enrichment — a technically demanding process requiring industrial centrifuge cascades under international safeguards (NPT, IAEA).

Uranium is dense (19.1 g/cm³), weakly radioactive, and found in pitchblende ore. Major producers: Kazakhstan, Canada, Namibia, Australia. The IAEA monitors all civilian enrichment programs globally.
⚛️ Plutonium — Pu-239
Plutonium is element 94 — it does not occur naturally in significant quantities. It is created in nuclear reactors when U-238 absorbs a neutron and decays. Pu-239 has a half-life of 24,100 years. It is highly toxic, carcinogenic, and more radioactive than uranium.

Plutonium is denser than uranium (19.8 g/cm³) and requires much smaller critical mass than U-235 — this is the basis of the implosion design used in the Fat Man bomb (Nagasaki, 1945), as documented in declassified US government records.

Civilian plutonium reprocessing is conducted by France, UK, Russia, Japan and China. All stockpiles are under IAEA safeguards in NPT signatory states. Global civilian Pu stockpile: ~310 metric tons as of 2023.
☢️ Nuclear Fission vs Fusion
Fission: Splitting heavy atoms (U-235 or Pu-239). A neutron strikes the nucleus, splitting it and releasing energy + 2–3 more neutrons → chain reaction. First achieved December 2, 1942 (Chicago Pile-1, Enrico Fermi). Basis of all nuclear reactors and first-generation weapons.

Fusion: Combining light atoms (hydrogen isotopes deuterium + tritium) at extreme temperatures (100 million °C). Releases far more energy per unit mass than fission. Powers the Sun. Basis of hydrogen bombs (thermonuclear weapons). Civilian fusion power (ITER project in France) aims for net energy gain — as of 2026, NIF achieved ignition milestone in 2022.

A thermonuclear weapon uses a fission primary to compress and ignite a fusion secondary. The yield can be thousands of times greater than a fission-only device.
🕊️ NPT & Arms Control
The Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT, 1968) recognizes 5 nuclear-weapon states (US, Russia, UK, France, China) and commits them to disarmament. 191 signatories. India, Pakistan, Israel never signed. North Korea withdrew in 2003.

Key treaties: SALT I/II (1972/1979), START I (1991), New START (2010, suspended by Russia 2023), INF Treaty (1987, US withdrew 2019), TPNW (Treaty on the Prohibition of Nuclear Weapons, 2021 — not signed by nuclear states).

Doomsday Clock (Bulletin of Atomic Scientists): 89 seconds to midnight as of 2024 — the closest to midnight since its creation in 1947.
